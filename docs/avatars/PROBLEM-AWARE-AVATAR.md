## 2. EXTERNAL CIRCUMSTANCES

Marcus runs a legitimate business. He has a team CRM (probably GHL, Follow Up Boss, or LionDesk), he's running Meta and Google ads, he's paying for Zillow leads, he has some kind of automation set up. On paper, the infrastructure exists.

But the *actual daily reality* looks like this:

- He's texting agents at 8pm asking if they followed up on the new leads from this morning
- He's manually reassigning leads when an agent doesn't respond fast enough
- He's getting calls from leads saying "someone was supposed to call me" — two days after they submitted a form
- His top agent (the one closing 40% of the team's volume) just had a conversation with a competing team about a better "system"
- He has 400+ leads in a "nurture" bucket that nobody has touched in 6 months
- He paid $8,400 last month between Zillow, Opcity, and Meta ads — and closed 3 deals from it

He tells himself it's a *people* problem. If the agents just followed up faster. If they just used the CRM the way he set it up. If they just worked the leads harder.

But somewhere, in quiet moments, he suspects it might be a *systems* problem.

## 4. PSYCHOGRAPHICS

### Identity
Marcus's identity is built around being a *builder*. He didn't just sell houses — he built a team. He's proud of that. He sees himself as a business owner, not an agent. He talks about "systems" and "leverage" because he learned that language from the coaching culture he runs in. He wants to build something that runs without him — that's the dream he sold himself when he started recruiting.

### Values
- **Independence** — He wants to own his production, not rent it from Zillow or a franchise
- **Control** — He wants to see what's happening at all times. He doesn't trust what he can't see.
- **Loyalty** — He's fiercely loyal to agents who perform. Losing a top agent feels personal.
- **Respect** — He's built something real. He wants to be taken seriously in his market.
- **Legacy** — Quietly, he thinks about what he's building and whether it'll still be around in 10 years

### Self-Image vs. Reality Gap
Marcus thinks of himself as a systems guy. But his current systems are mostly duct tape and willpower. He built them himself, by himself, and they worked when the team was 3 people. Now that it's 12 or 20 people, the same systems are showing cracks. He doesn't fully admit this yet — but he feels it.

## 5. THE EMOTIONAL LANDSCAPE

### Primary Emotions (Daily)
- **Frustrated** — He does everything right and still watches leads die
- **Anxious** — The top agent situation is always simmering. One bad month and the conversation changes.
- **Overwhelmed** — He's managing people, managing leads, managing ads, managing the brokerage relationship. He's the operator, not just the visionary.
- **Competitive** — He knows there are other teams in his market running ads. He wants to be the one with the unfair advantage.
- **Hopeful but skeptical** — He's been pitched CRMs and "AI solutions" before. Most of them were tools, not systems. He's open — but cautious.

### Primary Fear
**Losing his top producer.**

This is the existential threat. Marcus knows exactly which 2–3 agents are carrying the team. If one of them leaves — especially to a competing team that promises better leads, better systems, better everything — the business doesn't just lose a producer. The culture shifts. The other agents notice. The revenue drops. And Marcus has to start over.

This fear lives in the back of his mind on every good day and the front of his mind on every bad one.

### Secondary Fear
**Wasting money on leads that don't convert.**

He's spending $5K–$15K/month on lead generation. The ROI is unclear. He knows leads are being lost — he just doesn't know exactly where, how often, or how much it's costing him. This uncertainty drives him crazy.

### The Buried Resentment
He's been let down by vendors. Zillow sells him leads and then sells the same leads to 4 other agents. Opcity calls it "exclusive" but the leads have already talked to someone else. The ad agency he hired ran his Meta campaigns for 6 months and produced a detailed report with zero accountability for actual closings. He's tired of paying for the *appearance* of a system.

**Emotional job:** Feel like a real business owner, not a babysitter. Feel like his system is running *for* him, not demanding constant attention from him.

**Social job:** Be the team in his market that competitors whisper about. Be the team that top agents *want* to join because of the infrastructure. Be able to say, "Our system handles that."

## 7. INFORMATION BEHAVIOR

**Where he researches solutions:**
- YouTube: searches "real estate team systems," "lead follow-up automation real estate," "AI for real estate teams"
- Facebook Groups: Real estate team leader groups, GHL user groups
- Peer referrals: "Who are you using for leads?" at masterminds, conferences (Tom Ferry Summit, Inman)
- Direct outreach: He responds to well-crafted DMs if the hook is relevant and specific

**What earns his attention:**
- Specificity ("teams doing $5M–$20M GCI") over generality ("real estate agents")
- Proof of results — not screenshots, but stories. "One of our clients was down a top producer and within 60 days..."
- A founder who clearly understands the *real estate team* context, not just "AI" or "automation" in the abstract
- A real number (appointments booked, response time, conversion rate) in the first sentence

**What kills his attention:**
- Corporate SaaS language ("leverage synergies," "omnichannel engagement")
- Generic AI claims without real estate specificity
- Complicated onboarding or long implementation timelines
- Anyone who can't explain clearly why this is different from the last 3 things he bought

## 8. PURCHASING BEHAVIOR

**Decision style:** Research-first, then a conversation. Marcus doesn't click "Buy Now" on $5K/mo software. He books a call. He asks hard questions. He wants to talk to someone who understands his market.

**Decision timeline:** 1–4 weeks from first awareness to buying decision, if the trust signals are right. He moves faster if there's a genuine market exclusivity mechanic (his market could get taken).

**Who influences him:**
- Peers in mastermind groups
- Coaching community (Tom Ferry, Craig Procter circles)
- His highest-performing agent's opinion ("Would *you* use this?")

**Price sensitivity:** Low-to-moderate at the offer level. He'll pay $4,997/mo if the ROI math is clear. He will not pay $997/mo for "another CRM." The framing matters more than the number.

**The DBR Entry Point:** The $1,500 Database Revival offer is perfect for Marcus. It's low-risk, fast-result, and speaks directly to the $8K+ of leads already sitting dead in his CRM. He doesn't need to believe in the whole system yet — he just needs to see one win.
